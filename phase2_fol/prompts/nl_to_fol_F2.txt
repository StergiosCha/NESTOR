=== PROVER9 SYNTAX ===

- Universal: all x (P(x) -> Q(x))
- Existential: exists x (P(x) & Q(x))
- Connectives: & (and), | (or), - (not), -> (implies), <-> (iff)
- Equality: =
- Variables: lowercase (x, y, z, e, e1, e2)
- Constants: lowercase (john, mary)
- Predicates: lowercase (run, agent, patient)

=== DAVIDSONIAN EVENT SEMANTICS ===

Core idea: every verb introduces an event variable. Modifiers, agents,
patients are all separate conjuncts on that event. This makes modification
compositional: adding "quickly" is just adding quick(e), not changing the
whole predicate.

--- Thematic roles ---

agent(e, x)      the one who acts (subjects of active verbs)
patient(e, x)    the one affected (objects of active verbs)
theme(e, x)      what is moved/transferred (objects of give, send, throw)
goal(e, x)       destination or recipient (indirect objects)
location(e, x)   where the event happens
instrument(e, x) what is used to do it

--- How to translate ---

1. INTRANSITIVE VERBS: introduce event, add agent
   "John runs"
   -> exists e (run(e) & agent(e, john))

   "Some man walks"
   -> exists x exists e (man(x) & walk(e) & agent(e, x))

   "Every student sleeps"
   -> all x (student(x) -> exists e (sleep(e) & agent(e, x)))

2. TRANSITIVE VERBS: event + agent + patient
   "John loves Mary"
   -> exists e (love(e) & agent(e, john) & patient(e, mary))

   "Every man reads a book"
   -> all x (man(x) -> exists y exists e (book(y) & read(e) & agent(e, x) & patient(e, y)))

3. DITRANSITIVE VERBS: event + agent + theme + goal
   "John gave Mary a book"
   -> exists e exists x (give(e) & agent(e, john) & theme(e, x) & book(x) & goal(e, mary))

4. ADJECTIVES: 1-place predicates on entities, NOT on events
   "John is tall"         -> tall(john)
   "A tall man runs"      -> exists x exists e (man(x) & tall(x) & run(e) & agent(e, x))

5. ADVERBS / MANNER: 1-place predicates on events
   "John runs quickly"    -> exists e (run(e) & agent(e, john) & quick(e))
   "John carefully opened the door"
   -> exists e (open(e) & agent(e, john) & patient(e, door1) & careful(e))

6. LOCATIVES and TEMPORALS: predicates on events
   "John runs in the park"
   -> exists e (run(e) & agent(e, john) & location(e, park1))

7. PASSIVES: swap agent/patient
   "The book was read by John"
   -> exists e (read(e) & agent(e, john) & patient(e, book1))
   Same as active, just the roles tell you who did what.

8. NEGATION: negate the event existence
   "John does not run"
   -> -exists e (run(e) & agent(e, john))

   "No man walks"
   -> all x (man(x) -> -exists e (walk(e) & agent(e, x)))

9. STATIVES: "is", "has", copular sentences
   "John is a student"   -> student(john)          (no event needed)
   "John is tall"         -> tall(john)             (no event needed)
   "John has a car"       -> exists x (car(x) & have(john, x))  (or use event if you prefer)

   Rule: use events for dynamic verbs, skip events for pure statives
   (is, has, belongs to). If in doubt, use an event.

10. QUANTIFIERS (same as F1)
    "All A are B"          -> all x (A(x) -> B(x))
    "Some A are B"         -> exists x (A(x) & B(x))
    "No A is B"            -> all x (A(x) -> -B(x))
    "Most A are B"         -> approximate: exists x (A(x) & B(x))
    "The A"                -> exists x (A(x) & all y (A(y) -> y = x) & ...)

11. RELATIVE CLAUSES: conjoin inside the quantifier
    "Every man who runs is tired"
    -> all x ((man(x) & exists e (run(e) & agent(e, x))) -> tired(x))

12. MULTI-PREMISE ITEMS: same vocabulary across all premises
    Every premise becomes a separate formula. Use the same predicate
    names and constants everywhere.

=== CONSISTENCY RULES ===

- ONE predicate per concept across all formulas. Never man(x) in one
  formula and male(x) in another.
- Arity must be consistent: if love(e) in one formula, always love(e).
- Thematic roles are always 2-place: agent(e, x), patient(e, x).
- Event variables: e, e1, e2. Entity variables: x, y, z, u, v, w.
- Constants for named entities: john, mary, bill (not j, m, b).

=== WORKED EXAMPLES ===

Example 1 (entailment, FraCaS-style):
  Premise: "Every man who runs sees a dog"
  Hypothesis: "Some man sees a dog"

  Premises:
  all x ((man(x) & exists e1 (run(e1) & agent(e1, x))) -> exists y exists e2 (dog(y) & see(e2) & agent(e2, x) & patient(e2, y)))
  Hypothesis:
  exists x exists y exists e (man(x) & dog(y) & see(e) & agent(e, x) & patient(e, y))

Example 2 (contradiction):
  Premise: "No Italian runs"
  Hypothesis: "Some Italian runs"

  Premises:
  all x (italian(x) -> -exists e (run(e) & agent(e, x)))
  Hypothesis:
  exists x exists e (italian(x) & run(e) & agent(e, x))

Example 3 (multi-premise entailment):
  Premise: "Every European is a person. Every Italian is a European. John is an Italian."
  Hypothesis: "John is a person"

  Premises:
  all x (european(x) -> person(x))
  all x (italian(x) -> european(x))
  italian(john)
  Hypothesis:
  person(john)

Example 4 (with modifier, entailment):
  Premise: "John runs quickly"
  Hypothesis: "John runs"

  Premises:
  exists e (run(e) & agent(e, john) & quick(e))
  Hypothesis:
  exists e (run(e) & agent(e, john))

  Note: this works because Davidsonian semantics makes the inference
  automatic. "runs quickly" has run(e) as a conjunct, so "runs" follows
  by conjunction elimination. This is the main advantage of Davidsonian
  over plain predicate logic.

Example 5 (passive, entailment):
  Premise: "The book was read by John"
  Hypothesis: "John read something"

  Premises:
  exists e (read(e) & agent(e, john) & patient(e, book1))
  Hypothesis:
  exists x exists e (read(e) & agent(e, john) & patient(e, x))

=== WHY DAVIDSONIAN? ===

Plain FOL: "John runs quickly" -> runs_quickly(john)
           "John runs"         -> runs(john)
           No logical relation between runs_quickly(john) and runs(john)!
           The prover cannot infer one from the other.

Davidsonian: "John runs quickly" -> exists e (run(e) & agent(e,john) & quick(e))
             "John runs"         -> exists e (run(e) & agent(e,john))
             The inference is just conjunction elimination. Automatic.

This matters for FraCaS sections with modifiers, manner adverbs,
locatives, and temporal expressions. Without events, the prover is blind
to these inferences.
