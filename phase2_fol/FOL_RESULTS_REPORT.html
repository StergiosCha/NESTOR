1. The Story So Far

We built a pipeline that takes NLI pairs (premise + hypothesis), translates them into First-Order Logic via an LLM, and then uses automated theorem provers (Prover9 and MACE4) to determine the inference relation: Entailment, Contradiction, or Unknown.

We ran this pipeline across 5 datasets and 9 models, producing 23,670 inference attempts. The headline numbers look like this:

The top model is grok-4-20-reasoning, consistently outperforming others by 5-10 points. But even the best model gets only ~69% on FraCaS and ~63% on OYXOY. The bottleneck is not the theorem prover — it's the quality of the FOL translation.

2. How the Pipeline Works

The pipeline runs 4 phases in order. It exits as soon as it gets a definitive answer:

PhaseToolWhat It ChecksIf Success
AProver9P ⊢ H (can we prove H from P?)Entailment — exit
BProver9P ⊢ ¬H (can we prove ¬H from P?)Contradiction — exit
CMACE4P ∧ ¬H satisfiable? (find a model)Record found_c = True/False
DMACE4P ∧ H satisfiable? (find a model)Record found_d = True/False

After Phases C and D:

found_cfound_dLabelMeaning
model foundmodel foundUnknownP is compatible with both H and ¬H — it doesn't decide
anything elseUndecidedThe tools couldn't determine the relation (timeout, domain too small)

Key insight: Prover9 gives definitive answers (universal proofs). MACE4 gives existential evidence (finds a finite model). When MACE4 doesn't find a model, it doesn't mean unsatisfiable — it might just need a larger domain.

4. Where Are We Getting It Wrong?

The big problem: The pipeline has a massive bias toward Unknown.
  • 57% of gold Contradictions are misclassified as Unknown
  • 41% of gold Entailments are misclassified as Unknown

This is not a pipeline logic bug — the decision tree is correct. The problem is that when the FOL translation doesn't capture the semantic relationship between P and H, the predicates are independent, and MACE4 can trivially find models for both P∧¬H and P∧H → Unknown.

OYXOY is the worst: only 14% of contradictions are caught. Two-thirds become Unknown. The reason: OYXOY items use natural language that relies on world knowledge and antonymy (e.g., “stale” vs “fresh”), which the FOL translation doesn't capture.

6. Error Taxonomy: Why Does It Fail?

Classification of all 11,542 wrong answers (with FOL available)

Error TypeCount%Explanation
Antonymy / semantic gap5,42349.6%P and H use related but unlinked predicates. stale(x) and fresh(x) are independent in FOL — no axiom says stale(x) → ¬fresh(x). MACE4 finds models for both, so the result is Unknown instead of Contradiction.
Prover9 timeout / failure1,01918.8%The FOL looks correct, but Prover9 couldn't find a proof within the time limit. Often happens with nested quantifiers or complex formulas that are provable but computationally expensive.
No shared predicates74213.7%P and H have zero predicates in common. The LLM used completely different predicate names for the same concept (e.g., bought(x) in P vs purchased(x) in H).
Generalized quantifier1472.7%“Most”, “few”, “at most” — these cannot be expressed in standard FOL. The LLM translates them as all or exists, which changes the meaning.
Other82715.2%Miscellaneous: wrong quantifier scope, incorrect negation placement, structural errors.
Key takeaway: ~50% of errors come from the FOL translation failing to capture semantic relationships (antonymy, synonymy, hypernymy). This is the single biggest area for improvement. Adding background axioms (e.g., stale(x) ↔ ¬fresh(x)) or using a more semantically aware translation strategy could cut the error rate in half.

7. Concrete Examples

Example 1: Antonymy not captured (OYXOY)

Gold: Contradiction → Pipeline: Unknown

P: “Δεν ξαναψωνίζω από εκείνο το ζαχαροπλαστείο, μπαγιάτικα γλυκά μου πούλησε”

H: “Η Αφροδίτη ισχυρίστηκε πως τα γλυκά ήταν φρεσκότατα”

FOL P: sold(afroditi, shop, glyka) & stale(glyka)

FOL H: claimed(afroditi, fresh(glyka))

Problem: stale(glyka) and fresh(glyka) are independent predicates. MACE4 finds models for both P∧H and P∧¬H → Unknown. The fix would be adding an axiom: stale(x) → ¬fresh(x).

Example 2: Generalized quantifier “most” (FraCaS)

Gold: Entailment → Pipeline: Unknown (all 9 models fail)

P: “Most great tenors are Italian.”

H: “There are great tenors who are Italian.”

FOL P: all x ((great(x) & tenor(x)) → italian(x))

Problem: “Most” was translated as “all” (too strong) or as something that doesn't entail existence. In standard FOL, “most” simply cannot be expressed — it requires generalized quantifier theory.

Example 3: Prover9 should have proved this (FraCaS)

Gold: Entailment → Pipeline: Undecided (all 9 models fail)

P: “At least three tenors will take part in the concert.”

H: “There are tenors who will take part in the concert.”

FOL P: ∃x∃y∃z (tenor(x) & tenor(y) & tenor(z) & take_part(x,c) & take_part(y,c) & take_part(z,c) & x≠y & x≠z & y≠z)

FOL H: ∃x (tenor(x) & take_part(x,concert))

Problem: The FOL is correct and the proof is trivial (∃x,y,z ... ⇒ ∃x ...). But Prover9 timed out or there's a formatting issue (note: c vs concert — different constant names!). This is a bug to investigate.

Example 4: No shared predicates

Gold: Contradiction → Pipeline: Unknown

When the LLM uses bought(x, shop) in the premise but purchased(x, store) in the hypothesis, or grumbled(afroditi) vs complained(afroditi), Prover9 sees them as completely different predicates with no logical connection.

8. The Generalized Quantifier Wall

Standard FOL cannot express generalized quantifiers. Here's how the pipeline performs on FraCaS items containing each quantifier:

QuantifierItemsAccuracyNote
several7275%Often translatable as ∃x∃y (x≠y & ...)
many8169%Sometimes works when translated as ∃, sometimes not
at least4558%Numerical: ∃x∃y∃z ... with distinctness
few9057%“Few X are Y” — does it entail ∃x(X(x)∧Y(x))?
most9949%Coin-flip accuracy. Cannot express in FOL.
at most4544%Upper bounds are hard: ∀x∀y∀z (¬(all three satisfy...))
more than911%Essentially random.
less than90%Zero correct. Inherently beyond FOL.
This is a known theoretical limitation. FOL has exactly two quantifiers: ∀ and ∃. “Most”, “few”, “at most N”, “less than N” require second-order logic or generalized quantifier theory. Report this as a principled limitation of the FOL approach, not as a pipeline bug.

Temporal Reference is the hardest by far (35%). Temporal reasoning requires interval logic (Allen's interval algebra), which FOL can simulate but the LLM translations rarely get right. This is another principled limitation worth discussing.

12. What You Should Do Next

For the report, you need to:
  1. Reproduce these numbers — run the analysis scripts on your machine, verify you get the same accuracy figures. Don't copy numbers from this document.
  2. Break down accuracy by gold label — report precision/recall/F1 per label (Entailment, Contradiction, Unknown), not just overall accuracy.
  3. Analyze Undecided items — look at the steps_detail fields. How many are Prover9 timeouts vs FOL syntax errors vs something else?
  4. Investigate the “trivial proof” failures — items like fracas-0015 where the FOL is correct but Prover9 fails. Check the Prover9 input file formatting and timeout settings.
  5. Compare across conditions — which c1/c2/c3/c4 condition works best? Does few-shot prompting improve FOL translation quality?
  6. Report the theoretical limits honestly — generalized quantifiers, temporal logic, and non-intersective adjectives are principled limitations of FOL, not bugs. Frame them as such.
  7. Quantify the translation quality problem — the “no shared predicates” (12%) and “antonymy missing” (50%) categories are directly attributable to FOL translation quality. Can we improve this with better prompts, background axioms, or a verification step?
  8. Cross-lingual analysis — the EN vs EL comparison is a concrete result. Report which models are robust across languages and which aren't.
Specific analyses to run:
  1. Per-model precision/recall/F1 for each of the 3 labels, per dataset. Use sklearn.metrics.classification_report.
  2. Confusion matrices per model (not just aggregated). Which model is best at contradiction detection?
  3. Accuracy by FraCaS section × model. Does any model excel at temporal reasoning?
  4. Error analysis on a random sample of 50 wrong answers: manually categorize the FOL translation error. This gives you a human-validated error taxonomy.
  5. Compare the found_c/found_d values in steps_detail for Undecided items. How many would become Unknown vs stay Undecided under the new 4-phase tree?
What's working well:
  • The pipeline architecture is sound — when the FOL translation is good, the theorem prover gets the right answer.
  • Entailment detection on “simple” items (∀/∃ quantifiers, set inclusion) works reliably (70%+ on those subsets).
  • The 4-phase decision tree correctly handles all the edge cases we've identified.
  • Reasoning models (grok-reasoning, deepseek-r1) produce substantially better FOL translations.
